Automated Pricing Mechanism

For my Insight Data Science project, I teamed up with GoLorry, a trucking startup operating in India, to automate their pricing.

The Brief

GoLorry aims to match up shippers with drivers, and presents the shippers with instant pricing on their order to provide surety of cost and reduce the transaction costs for shippers.

Currently they are pricing manually based on their knowledge of the market. It is a time-intensive method, and is not sustainable as they continue to scale up. My role was to create an automated pricing mechanism based on their past orders.

The Pipeline

The project has four main components. First, a pricing algorithm is created that uses past orders to train a predictive price mechanism for new orders. Second, the shipper enters a the order details in an interactive form. Third, the algorithm presents the shipper with a price that the customer accepts or rejects. Finally a cosine similarity measure is used to predict whether the order might be an outlier, i.e. the predicted price is not likely to be accurate. If the order is similar to another recent order for which the cost was significantly greater than the price, a flag will be raised prompting GoLorry to take a closer look at the price.

The Pricing Algorithm

The main obstacle with this project was the scarcity of data. It was not possible to train a useful model using only completed orders with cost details. As a result, I chose to use the set of all priced orders, whether or not the customer accepted, in order to provide an automated version of the model that GoLorry is currently using. As the completed order dataset continues to grow they will be able to update the pricing mechanism using the python code I provided on only completed orders, and get a better fit with actual costs. Once they reach a sufficient level I would suggest transitioning to more of a machine learning approach to get a more flexible and accurate model.

For the automated pricing model I fit a linear regression to the priced order dataset, and predicted their pricing with 90% accuracy. I kept the features limited to avoid overfitting, and to ensure it would translate over into the completed order dataset.

Two features of interest were chillies and whether the shipment was headed to, or from, West Bengal. These showed up as significant in the regression, and upon further investigation I found that the reasons.

Chillies are very light, and as a result the trucks are often overloaded. These overloaded trucks are subject to fines as they cross state borders, but the fine is small enough to make it worthwhile. A shipment of chillies has an on average 14140 rupee surcharge according to the model.

The other interesting variable was West Bengal. There are more shipments that go to West Bengal than shipments leaving West Bengal. As a result, because drivers' costs are higher if there is no return fare, a shipment with the destination of West Bengal has a premium of 27000 rupees, and a shipment originating in West Bengal will be 17000 rupees cheaper, all else equal.

The pricing algorithm had an R-squared (fit) of 75% when applied to the completed order data. The fitted model slightly overestimates the price, due to the inherent bias in the priced dataset, where on average the prices for orders that were not accepted was slightly higher. Ideally this model would be used only on completed order data in the future, to get a better estimate of the true cost of an order. However, we should keep in mind that even these data are skewed, as the price was set by GoLorry, and they had a general strategy of taking a fixed margin, and as a result the true cost may not have been revealed.
